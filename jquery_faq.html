National Parks:

Q: Who is the director of the National Park Service?
A: David Vela is the Deputy Director, exercising the authority of the Director of the National Park Service. Learn more about past directors of the National Park Service.

Q: What government agency oversees the National Park Service?
A: The National Park Service is a bureau of the Department of the Interior. Directly overseeing its operation is the department's Assistant Secretary for Fish, Wildlife and Parks.

Q: How many employees are in the National Park Service?
A:
  • Permanent, temporary, and seasonal employees: Approximately 20,000
  • Volunteers: More than 279,000 in 2019

Q: How old is the National Park System?
A: The National Park Service was created by an act signed by President Woodrow Wilson on August 25, 1916. Yellowstone National Park was established by an act signed by President Ulysses S. Grant on March 1, 1872, as the nation's first national park. View the National Park System timeline.

Q: What is the origin of the National Park Service arrowhead?
A: The arrowhead was authorized as the official National Park Service emblem by the Secretary of the Interior on July 20, 1951. The components of the arrowhead may have been inspired by key attributes of the National Park System, with the sequoia tree and bison representing vegetation and wildlife, the mountains and water representing scenic and recreational values, and the arrowhead itself representing historical and archeological values. Read more about the history of the arrowhead and other elements of NPS visual design.

Q: How many areas are in the National Park System?
A: The system includes 419 areas covering more than 85 million acres in every state, the District of Columbia, American Samoa, Guam, Puerto Rico, and the Virgin Islands. These areas include national parks, monuments, battlefields, military parks, historical parks, historic sites, lakeshores, seashores, recreation areas, scenic rivers and trails, and the White House. Learn more about national park designations. See the complete list of National Park Service units and related areas by type and number.

Q: What is the largest national park site? Smallest?
A:
  • Largest: Wrangell-St. Elias National Park and Preserve, AK, at 13.2 million acres
  • Smallest: Thaddeus Kosciuszko National Memorial, PA, at 0.02 acres

Q: How many people visit the national parks?
A: Total recreation visitors to the national parks in 2019: 327,516,619.

Yellowstone National Park

  • Yellowstone encompasses 3,472 square miles (2,221,766 acres) which makes it larger than Rhode Island and Delaware combined.
  • Yellowstone is home to more than 500 active geysers (more than half the world’s geysers).
  • Yellowstone is home to the largest concentration of mammals in the lower 48 states (67 species).
  • There are approximately 1,000 miles of hiking trails in Yellowstone.

Yosemite National Park

  • Located in the Sierra Nevada Mountains of California, Yosemite National Park spans 761,268 acres of land
  • Before it became a national park, Yosemite had already been under government protection since 1864, as signed by President Lincoln. This was the first time land became secured by the government due to its natural beauty, to be preserved for people to enjoy.

Zion National Park

  • The person who is credited with naming the place “Zion” is also the same person who, in 1863, settled the canyon floor. His name is Isaac Behunin.
  • 1917 was the first year tourists arrived here by way of automobile.
  • The first recognized and exhibited paintings of Zion were created by artist Frederick Dellenbaugh who displayed his work at the St. Louis World’s Fair in 1904.
  • The Anasazi, the original occupants of Zion Canyon, have left behind rock art and many cliff dwellings that are between 800 and 1,500 years old and can still be seen today.
